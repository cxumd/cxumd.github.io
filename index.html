Variation in Agricultural Value Between Continents¶

Zan Xu & Thomas Chung¶

Does agriculture have the same effect between continents? And does it form the same proportion of GDP?

In this tutorial, we demonstrate a very strong association between both agricultural product and GDP, which is not very surprising. But the association between the two is very heavily based on geographic location. In fact, it is possible to predict the continent of a country purely by looking at agricultural and macroeconomic indicators, despite neither of the two alone being sufficient to discern.

Gathering Data¶

Most of our data is pulled from the UN's Food and Agriculture Organization stats site, or FAOSTAT. For economic metrics that cannot be found on FAOSTAT, we use data from the world bank.

The desired files were separated from the relevant archives before being uploaded onto a GCP static storage bucket. For convenience and to avoid repeatedly pulling from the sources, we pull from this bucket instead.

The FAOSTAT data can be found at http://www.fao.org/faostat/en/#data. The filenames start with the sections they can be found in. The datasets can be found in a zipped folders from the "All Data" bulk download, with the same filename as on the GCP bucket. (It might also need to be re-encoded as latin-1.)

The various caveats of the data can also be found on the website. The most important factor for us is that crops grown for animal husbandry are counted separately, which we will have to fill in later. Also, harvesting wild plants and subsistence farming are probably underreported.

We will start with the data on the GCP buckets, fetching the data and dropping some columns we won't use:

Parsing Data¶

As can be seen from the preview, the NaN values represent points from before a crop was introduced to an area. In particular there is no data where a nonzero but unknown amount of agriculture is filled as NaN, since the FAO inserts expert provide estimates wherever official figures are not available. So every NaN in our dataframe really represents zero.

The table measures production in 3 different ways, but we are only looking at production quantity. So we do not need the other methods of measurement. The table gives the annual production of an individual crop for every country, so because we are looking at the total crop production in each country, we add up the production of all the crops in each country.

What we found is that the agricultural production and GPV are also significantly affected by the continent. As a matter of fact, we can predict the continent of a country purely by its production and gpv:

from sklearn.cluster import Birch
cluster_data = data[["Production (Tonnes)", "Gross Production Value (Million $,2010)"]].copy()
cluster_data.columns = ["x","y"]
cluster_data["y"] = cluster_data["y"] * 4e3 
# We scale the y-axis to be a slightly larger scale than the x-axis, so that our metric considers variation in GPV slightly more important, 
# instead of ~1000 times less, if it went by pure magnitude alone.
cluster = Birch(n_clusters=6).fit_predict(cluster_data)
# map and agglomerate clusters
cluster[cluster == 4] = 2
cluster[cluster == 5] = 4
plt.scatter(x=cluster_data["x"],y=cluster_data["y"], c=cluster)
plt.show()

We use the BIRCH algorithm, instead of K-means. Why? The important part is that our data is both extremely anisotropic -- that is, it forms linear bands -- and that it has very highly uneven density. K-means likes to have roughly circular clusters of roughly equal population, meaning that it is unsuitable for our application.

We tried many different clustering algorithms, but settled on BIRCH because it handled both of these factors well. In particular, most algorithms tended to either group the two middle clusters together (anisotropy) or break up the top cluster into two separate clusters (density). For more info, a comparison of different clustering algos in scikit-learn can be found in https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/clustering.html.

This correlation is in much due part of the fact that GPV is FAOSTAT's own calculation. However, the correlation between GDP and Production is not as close.
